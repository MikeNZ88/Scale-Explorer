üé∏ Practical Guitar Voicing

In practice, guitarists use note omission to create playable voicings. Here's the established hierarchy:

1
Fifth (5th) - First to Omit

The fifth is "harmonically inert" and doesn't define major/minor quality. It's implied in the overtones of the root.

2
Root - Context Dependent

Can be omitted when playing with a bass player or other harmonic instruments that provide the root.

3
Extensions (9th, 11th) - Situational

For 13th chords, the 9th and 11th can be omitted while retaining the characteristic 13th extension.

üö´
Never Omit: Third and Seventh

The 3rd defines major/minor quality. The 7th determines dominant vs. major 7th character. These are essential for chord identity.

For maj6 chords, you must include the fifth to avoid sounding like the relative minor triad. Cmaj6 without the 5th (C-E-A) sounds like Am (A-C-E).

üé® Color Theory Foundation

The interval color system is based on academic research in music psychology, cross-modal perception, and color theory. Colors represent the harmonic tension and emotional characteristics of each interval:

  • White (Root): Complete stability and resolution - the gravitational center of all harmony
  • Yellow (Major Third): Brightness and happiness - defines major chord quality
  • Blue (Perfect Fifth): Stability and trust - acoustically stable (3:2 ratio)
  • Red (Perfect Fourth): Structural tension and suspension - demands resolution
  • Dark Red (Tritone): Maximum harmonic danger - the "devil's interval"
  • Purple (Minor Third): Sadness and emotional depth - defines minor chord quality
  • Orange (Seconds): Movement and mild tension - creates forward motion
  • Green (Major Sixth): Yearning and natural beauty - hopeful longing

This system synthesizes research from:

  • Music theory research on interval emotional characteristics
  • Color psychology studies on universal color-emotion associations
  • Cross-modal perception research on music-color mappings
  • Educational research on color-enhanced learning
