üé∏ Practical Guitar Voicing

In practice, guitarists use note omission to create playable voicings. Here's the established hierarchy:

1
Fifth (5th) - First to Omit

The fifth is "harmonically inert" and doesn't define major/minor quality. It's implied in the overtones of the root.

2
Root - Context Dependent

Can be omitted when playing with a bass player or other harmonic instruments that provide the root.

3
Extensions (9th, 11th) - Situational

For 13th chords, the 9th and 11th can be omitted while retaining the characteristic 13th extension.

üö´
Rarely Omit: Third and Seventh

The 3rd defines major/minor quality. The 7th determines dominant vs. major 7th character. These are essential for chord identity.

Interval Spelling & Colour Guide

Understanding Interval Spelling

In music theory, intervals must follow the consecutive numbering rule. For 7-note scales (like major scale, harmonic minor scale, and melodic minor scale), every scale degree (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) must be represented exactly once.

‚úÖ Correct (C Natural Minor / Aeolian):

Notes: C, D, E‚ô≠, F, G, A‚ô≠, B‚ô≠
(each number 1-7 used once)
‚ùå Incorrect (same C Natural Minor scale):
Intervals: 1, 2, ‚ôØ2, 4, 5, ‚ôØ5, ‚ô≠7
Notes (wrong): C, D, D‚ôØ, F, G, G‚ôØ, B‚ô≠
(missing 3 & 6, has duplicate 2 & 5)
üìù Different Scale Types:
  • 7-note scales (Major scale, Harmonic minor scale, Melodic minor scale): Use all 7 scale degrees (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) exactly once
  • 5-note scales (Pentatonic): Derived from major scale with missing intervals. Pentatonic omits 4 and 7 (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 5, 6)
  • 6-note scales (Blues, Whole Tone): Blues scales are pentatonic scales with added chromatic passing tones. Whole tone scales use 6 consecutive scale degrees with all intervals being whole steps
  • 8-note scales (Diminished/Octatonic): Too large for 1-7 system, may repeat numbers with different accidentals (e.g., both ‚ô≠6 and 6)

The key principle: interval spelling determines note spelling using consecutive letters. Exceptions: Pentatonic scales skip certain letters to match their missing intervals, and diminished scales may repeat letters due to their size exceeding the 7-letter system.

Why Learn Guitar Scales: Master the Major Scale First

The Core Problem Most Guitarists Face

Most guitarists struggle with scales because they approach them backwards. They try to learn:

  • Natural minor, harmonic minor, dorian, mixolydian as completely separate scales
  • Dozens of chord formulas with no connection between them
  • Scale patterns as isolated fretboard shapes
  • Modes as entirely different systems

This leads to years of confusion and slow progress.

The Solution: One Universal Reference Point

Learn the major scale first ‚Üí everything else clicks into place.

The major scale isn't just another scale - it's the foundation of all Western music. Every other scale, chord, and musical concept is defined by how it relates to the major scale.

Instead of memorizing everything separately, you learn:

  • One reference scale that you can move anywhere on the fretboard
  • One interval framework that explains all other intervals
  • The connections between scales, chords, and progressions
  • The logic behind why certain note and chord combinations work

What Is the Major Scale?

The major scale is a sequence of 7 notes following this pattern of whole steps (W) and half steps (H):

W - W - H - W - W - W - H

Example in C major: C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C

The scale covers one octave (12 semitones) from the root note back to the same note at a higher pitch. While there are technically only 7 unique scale degrees, we conventionally include the octave as the 8th degree.

Guitar-Specific Interval Shapes

Along one string: 1 semitone = 1 fret

  • Perfect 5th (7 semitones) = 7 frets higher on the same string

Across strings: Most interval shapes remain consistent across string pairs

  • Perfect 5th example: From the low E string (8th fret = C) to the A string (10th fret = G) creates a perfect 5th interval - this is the power chord shape! Power chords work everywhere on the fretboard because they maintain the same perfect 5th interval relationship
  • Exception: String pairs involving the B string require adjustment due to its different tuning - the same perfect 5th shape would need to move up one fret when crossing to or from the B string

Why the Major Scale Is Your Universal Reference

Every other musical element is described by how it differs from the major scale:

Other Scales
  • Natural minor = major scale with ‚ô≠3, ‚ô≠6, ‚ô≠7
  • Harmonic minor = natural minor with raised 7th
  • Dorian mode = major scale with ‚ô≠3, ‚ô≠7
  • Mixolydian = major scale with ‚ô≠7
Chord Construction
  • Minor chord = major chord with ‚ô≠3
  • Dominant 7 = major triad + ‚ô≠7
  • Minor 7 = minor triad + ‚ô≠7

This approach makes memorization exponentially easier - instead of learning dozens of unrelated formulas, you learn one reference point and simple modifications.

The Two Key Types That Dominate Music

Most songs use one of two key types:

Major Keys
Based on the major scale
Bright, resolved sound
Example: C major uses the C major scale
Minor Keys
Based on the natural minor scale (relative minor of major)
Darker, more emotional sound
Example: A minor uses the A natural minor scale (same notes as C major)
Key insight: The major scale and its relative natural minor use identical notes. Master the major scale patterns, and you instantly understand both key types.

What Are Modes? (Spoiler: You Already Know Them)

Modes sound intimidating, but they're simply the major scale starting from different notes. Each mode uses the exact same notes as the major scale, just with a different starting point (root).

The 7 modes of C major:
  • Ionian: C-D-E-F-G-A-B (starts on 1st degree) - This is just the modal name for the major scale
  • Dorian: D-E-F-G-A-B-C (starts on 2nd degree)
  • Phrygian: E-F-G-A-B-C-D (starts on 3rd degree)
  • Lydian: F-G-A-B-C-D-E (starts on 4th degree)
  • Mixolydian: G-A-B-C-D-E-F (starts on 5th degree)
  • Aeolian: A-B-C-D-E-F-G (starts on 6th degree) - This is just the modal name for the natural minor scale
  • Locrian: B-C-D-E-F-G-A (starts on 7th degree)

Why Different Roots Change Everything

Here's the crucial concept: When you change the root note, the interval relationships change completely.

Example using A and C:

  • In C major (Ionian): A is the 6th degree, C is the root (1st) ‚Üí A to C = 6 to 1
  • In A minor (Aeolian): A is the root (1st), C is the 3rd degree ‚Üí A to C = 1 to ‚ô≠3

Same notes (A and C), but completely different interval relationships depending on which note you treat as the root.

Common Modes You'll Actually Use

  • Dorian: Minor scale with a brighter sound (natural 6th instead of ‚ô≠6)
  • Mixolydian: Major scale with a bluesy edge (‚ô≠7 instead of major 7th)
  • Aeolian: The natural minor scale you already know

The other modes are less common in popular music, so focus on these three first.

Your Next Steps

  1. Master the major scale pattern in one key (start with C major - learning C major is just learning the natural note names on the fretboard, which helps with remembering note positions)
  2. Learn the interval relationships within that scale
  3. Review the related modes - practice starting the same scale pattern from each degree to hear how the intervals change when you shift the root
  4. Change the root note and watch how the note names shift but the fretboard patterns remain the same
  5. Apply the pattern to other keys across the fretboard
  6. Use it as your reference point for understanding all other scales and chords
Remember: The major scale isn't just another scale to learn - it's the key that unlocks everything else in music. Invest the time to truly understand it, and watch how quickly everything else falls into place.
